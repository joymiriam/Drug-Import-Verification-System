Common Diseases & Treatments

Disease Symptoms Treatment
Malaria Fever, chills, nausea, headaches Artemisinin-based combination therapy (ACT), including drugs such as Artesunate-Amodiaquine. Early treatment is crucial to prevent complications
Diabetes Frequent urination, increased thirst, weight loss Management includes lifestyle changes, insulin therapy, and oral medications. Regular monitoring of blood sugar levels is essential.
Hypertension High blood pressure, headaches, dizziness Medications include ACE inhibitors, Beta-blockers, and Calcium channel blockers. Lifestyle changes such as a low-sodium diet and regular exercise are recommended.
Asthma Shortness of breath, wheezing, chest tightness Long-term control medications such as corticosteroid inhalers and quick-relief inhalers help manage symptoms. Avoiding triggers is also crucial.
Influenza Fever, cough, sore throat, muscle aches Antiviral medications such as Oseltamivir and Zanamivir can reduce symptoms and duration. Rest, hydration, and over-the-counter fever reducers help alleviate discomfort
COVID-19 Fever, cough, difficulty breathing, loss of taste or smell Supportive care includes oxygen therapy and fever management. Antiviral drugs such as Remdesivir are used for severe cases. Vaccination remains the best preventive measure.
Tuberculosis Persistent cough, night sweats, weight loss A combination of antibiotics taken for at least six months. Completing the full course is essential to prevent drug resistance

Banned Drugs in Kenya

The Pharmacy and Poisons Board (PPB) is the national authority in Kenya responsible for regulating medicines and ensuring they are safe, effective, and of good quality. Sometimes, certain drugs are found to be harmful or no longer meet safety standards. When that happens, they are either suspended, withdrawn, or banned from the market.

Why are drugs banned?

Drugs may be banned in Kenya for several reasons, including:

  • Serious side effects or long-term health risks
  • No proven medical benefit
  • Potential for misuse or addiction
  • Substandard or counterfeit ingredients
  • Global safety alerts from WHO or other health agencies

Once a drug is banned, it is illegal to import, sell, distribute, or use it in Kenya. All importers, pharmacists, and healthcare professionals are expected to stay updated with the latest information from the PPB to ensure compliance and protect public health.
